# Writing Style Guide

Voice, tone, structure, and mechanics for all written content on MindPrism — chapter summaries, mental model cards, shorts, audio scripts, and UI copy.

---

## 1. Voice and Tone

### Core Voice Attributes
- **Intelligent but accessible.** Write for a curious professional, not an academic journal.
- **Direct.** Lead with the insight, not the preamble.
- **Warm but not casual.** Friendly and encouraging; never sloppy or overly informal.
- **Empowering.** Every piece of content should leave the reader feeling they can act on what they learned.

### Tone by Content Type

| Content Type | Tone |
|-------------|------|
| Chapter summaries | Engaging, precise, slightly conversational |
| Mental model cards | Crisp, structured, action-oriented |
| Audio scripts | Conversational, narrative, listen-first |
| Shorts | Punchy, declarative, scroll-stopping |
| Push notifications | Urgent but friendly, never alarmist |
| Email subjects | Curiosity-driven or benefit-led |
| UI microcopy | Minimal, clear, reassuring |

---

## 2. Person and Tense

- **Person:** Second-person ("you", "your"). Not first-person ("I") or third-person ("the reader").
- **Tense:** Present tense for summaries and mental models. ("The author argues" → "The core idea is...")
- **Exception:** Historical events and author context may use past tense.

**Wrong:** "Kahneman argued that humans have two systems of thinking."
**Right:** "Your brain runs on two systems — and understanding them changes how you make every decision."

---

## 3. Sentence and Paragraph Rules

- **Sentence length:** Mix short (under 12 words) and medium (12–25 words). Avoid sentences over 35 words.
- **Paragraph length:** 2–4 sentences max. No walls of text.
- **One idea per paragraph.** If a paragraph has two separate ideas, split it.
- **Avoid filler openers:** Never start with "In this chapter...", "The author...", "This book is about...", or "Basically..."
- **Front-load the insight.** The first sentence of every paragraph should contain the most important idea.

---

## 4. Banned Phrases and Words

| Banned | Replace With |
|--------|-------------|
| "In this chapter..." | Lead with the insight directly |
| "The author argues that..." | State the claim as a fact or "The core idea:" |
| "Basically" | Cut it |
| "Very", "really", "quite" | Cut or replace with a stronger word |
| "Utilize" | "Use" |
| "Leverage" (as a verb) | "Use", "apply", "deploy" |
| "In order to" | "To" |
| "It is important to note..." | State the important thing |
| "Game-changer", "paradigm shift" | Describe the specific change |
| "Empower" (overused) | Describe the specific capability |

## 5. Chapter Summary Structure

Every chapter summary follows this four-part structure:

```
[Hook — 1 sentence]
Captures the chapter's single most surprising or important claim.

[Core Argument — 2–3 paragraphs]
Explains the chapter's logic, evidence, and framework.
Each paragraph = one supporting idea.

**Key Insight**
> A callout block (bolded or blockquote) that distills the chapter to one sentence.

[Practical Takeaway — 1–2 sentences]
Concrete, actionable: "Next time you [situation], try [specific action]."
```

**Length target:** 400–500 words for standard chapters. 300–380 for intro/outro chapters. 500–600 for dense theoretical chapters.

---

## 6. Mental Model Card Structure

```
## [Model Name]  ← 2–5 words, memorable

**From:** [Book Title], Chapter [N]

**In one line:** [One sentence. Subject + verb + outcome.]

**The idea:**
[2–3 sentences explaining the model and why it matters.]

**When to apply it:**
- [Situation 1]
- [Situation 2]
- [Situation 3]

**Example:**
[1–2 sentences showing the model in a real scenario.]

**Common misapplication:**
[1 sentence on how people misuse or over-apply this model.]
```

## 7. Audio Script Rules

- Write for ears, not eyes. Read aloud during drafting.
- Short sentences. Maximum 20 words.
- Use rhetorical questions to create forward momentum: "But here's the catch — what happens when..."
- Avoid parentheticals (they don't work in audio).
- Avoid lists of more than 3 items without narrative connective tissue.
- Signal transitions explicitly: "Here's idea two...", "Now, the surprising part..."
- Script structure: Hook (30s) → 5–7 ideas with transitions → Closing action prompt (30s).

---

## 8. Shorts Copy Rules

- Hook line: ≤12 words. Statement, not question. Surprising or counterintuitive.
- Body: 3–5 bullet points, each ≤15 words.
- Source: "From [Book Title] by [Author]"
- CTA: "Read the full summary in MindPrism →"

**Example hook:** "The busiest people make fewer decisions than you think."

---

## 9. Numbers and Data

- Spell out numbers one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above.
- Percentages always use the % symbol: 73%, not "seventy-three percent."
- Years always as numerals: 2012, not "two thousand twelve."
- Do not invent statistics. If using data from the source book, attribute it: "According to [Author]'s research..."

---

## 10. Inclusivity and Sensitivity

- Use gender-neutral language by default. "They/their" as singular pronoun.
- Avoid mental health stigma language: use "experience depression" not "suffer from depression."
- Avoid cultural assumptions in examples. Default to universally relatable scenarios.
- Flag any content from books that contain outdated research or contested claims with an editorial note.
